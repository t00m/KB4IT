== Introduction

Quantum computing harnesses the principles of quantum mechanics to process information in ways that classical computers cannot. Unlike classical bits, which exist as either 0 or 1, quantum bits — or qubits — can exist in multiple states simultaneously, opening the door to exponential speedups for certain computational problems.

== Qubits and Superposition

A qubit is the fundamental unit of quantum information. While a classical bit is binary, a qubit exists in a superposition of both ∣0⟩ and ∣1⟩ states until measured:

    ∣0⟩ State — Analogous to classical 0

    ∣1⟩ State — Analogous to classical 1

    Superposition — A linear combination α∣0⟩ + β∣1⟩, where |α|² + |β|² = 1

The power of superposition allows a quantum computer with n qubits to represent 2ⁿ states simultaneously, enabling massive parallelism in computation.

== Quantum Entanglement

Einstein famously described entanglement as "spooky action at a distance." When qubits become entangled, the state of one qubit instantaneously correlates with the state of another, regardless of the physical distance between them:

    Bell States — Maximally entangled quantum states

    Measurement Correlation — Measuring one entangled qubit immediately determines the state of its partner

    Non-locality — Entanglement enables quantum teleportation and superdense coding

Entanglement is the resource that enables quantum algorithms to outperform their classical counterparts.

== Quantum Gates and Circuits

Quantum computations are performed by applying quantum gates to qubits, analogous to logic gates in classical computing. Unlike classical gates, quantum gates are reversible:

    Pauli Gates (X, Y, Z) — Single-qubit rotations analogous to NOT operations

    Hadamard Gate (H) — Creates superposition from basis states

    CNOT Gate — Two-qubit gate that flips target qubit based on control qubit state

These gates combine to form quantum circuits, the building blocks of quantum algorithms.

== Shor's and Grover's Algorithms

Two algorithms demonstrate quantum supremacy:

    Shor's Algorithm — Factors large integers in polynomial time, threatening RSA encryption by reducing the factoring problem from exponential to polynomial complexity

    Grover's Algorithm — Searches unsorted databases in O(√N) time, providing a quadratic speedup over classical O(N) search

While large-scale fault-tolerant quantum computers remain years away, these algorithms prove quantum advantage is theoretically possible.

== Conclusion

Quantum computing represents a paradigm shift in computation, trading classical determinism for probabilistic quantum mechanics. As hardware improves and error correction matures, quantum computers will likely solve problems in cryptography, drug discovery, and materials science that remain intractable today.
